Why Build Custom
The case for owning our forecasting infrastructure.
check_circle 1. No Third-Party Tool Covers Our Full Needs
We evaluated Flieber, Netstock, Inventory Planner, Streamline (GMDH), Lokad, DemandCaster, and several others. The findings are detailed in the Solutions Deep Dive tab, but the summary is clear: no tool has a native Deacom connector. Commerce-native tools like Flieber excel at Shopify + Amazon but have zero manufacturing planning. ERP-centric tools like Netstock handle supply planning but lack deep e-commerce integrations. Not a single tool we evaluated supports organic product shelf life / FEFO modeling out of the box. Acme sits at the intersection of e-commerce, wholesale, and hand manufacturing — that intersection is too specific for any general-purpose tool.
check_circle 2. We Need Every Inch of Our Data in One Place
The forecasting model is only as good as the data feeding it. We need every single data point from every system — not a summary, not an aggregation, not whatever subset a third-party tool decides to import. Every Shopify order line item. Every Amazon settlement fee. Every Deacom work order, lot number, BOM, and ingredient purchase. Every inventory movement across every channel. A custom data lake gives us complete control over what data we ingest, how we transform it, and how we feed it into our models. Third-party tools limit you to their import schema and their data model — we'd be fitting our business into their box instead of building a system that fits ours.
check_circle 3. Manufacturing Constraints Are Core, Not an Add-On
Acme isn't a reseller — we manufacture every product by hand. That means demand forecasting without production planning is half an answer. Our system must know: what are our batch sizes, how much labor is available, which equipment is running, what ingredients are in stock or in season, and what's the shelf life of finished goods. The forecast must be constraint-aware from day one — it should never recommend producing something we can't actually make. No third-party forecasting tool integrates this deeply with a Deacom-based manufacturing operation.
check_circle 4. Organic Products Require Shelf Life Intelligence
Our products expire. That's a fundamental constraint that changes how forecasting works. The system needs lot-level expiration tracking, FEFO (First Expired, First Out) inventory allocation, waste prediction, and production scheduling that accounts for sell-through velocity vs. shelf life. Overproducing an organic product isn't just a capital problem — it's a waste problem. Wholesale partners require minimum shelf life remaining at shipment. This is a dimension that virtually every off-the-shelf forecasting tool ignores because they were built for conventional, non-perishable goods.
check_circle 5. Full Ownership = No Vendor Lock-In
When we build, we own everything — the code, the models, the data, the infrastructure. No vendor can raise prices on us, sunset a feature we depend on, or deprioritize our integration requests. The data lake we build becomes a strategic asset that serves forecasting today and powers analytics, reporting, and operational intelligence tomorrow. With a SaaS tool, we're renting access to a black box we can't customize, and switching later means starting from scratch.
check_circle 6. The Data Lake Serves More Than Forecasting
Once we have all of our data in one place, the value extends far beyond demand forecasting. The same data lake powers: true margin analysis by SKU by channel (COGS + fees + shipping), customer cohort analysis from Shopify, Amazon advertising ROI tied to actual profitability, wholesale partner performance tracking, raw material spend optimization, and executive dashboards that show the complete picture of the business. We're not just building a forecasting tool — we're building the data infrastructure Acme needs to operate as a data-driven company.

What We're Proposing to Build
The complete scope of the custom forecasting platform.
1. Centralized Data Lake
A unified data warehouse (Azure SQL Database) that ingests every data point from Shopify, Amazon, and Deacom. Every order, every SKU, every lot number, every ingredient purchase, every fee, every inventory movement. One source of truth for the entire business. The foundation everything else is built on.

2. Automated Data Pipelines
Real-time and batch pipelines pulling data from all three systems. Shopify via REST/GraphQL APIs with webhook-driven real-time updates. Amazon via SP-API with full financial reconciliation. Deacom via read-only SQL extraction with incremental sync. All pipelines monitored, validated, and self-healing.

3. Unified SKU Master
A single product identity system mapping Shopify Product IDs, Amazon ASINs, and Deacom Item Numbers. This is the keystone — without it, we can't aggregate demand across channels or match sales to production. Every product, variant, bundle, and kit mapped cleanly.

4. ML-Powered Demand Forecasting
A tiered forecasting engine: statistical baselines (StatsForecast) for the long tail of SKUs, gradient-boosted models (LightGBM) for top revenue drivers with rich features (promotions, seasonality, price, channel), and hierarchical reconciliation to ensure channel-level forecasts sum coherently. Every signal we can capture gets fed into the model — the more data, the better the predictions.

5. Manufacturing-Aware Planning
Demand forecasts translated into production requirements through BOM explosion. Checked against production capacity (labor, equipment, batch sizes), raw material availability, ingredient seasonality, and shelf life constraints. The output answers both "will it sell?" and "can we make it?" — and flags conflicts before they become problems.

6. Shelf Life & FEFO Intelligence
Lot-level expiration tracking, First-Expired-First-Out inventory allocation, waste prediction, and production scheduling that accounts for sell-through velocity. Prevents overproduction of perishable organic goods and ensures wholesale partners receive products with adequate shelf life remaining.

7. Executive Dashboards
Power BI dashboards: Forecast vs. Actuals, Channel Sales, Inventory Health, Production Planning, Margin by SKU by Channel, Amazon FBA intelligence. Role-based access — executives see everything, teams see what's relevant to them.

8. Continuous Learning & Monitoring
Automated model retraining, accuracy monitoring, data drift detection, and pipeline health alerts. The system gets smarter over time as it ingests more data. When accuracy degrades, it flags the issue and retrains. No manual intervention needed for routine operation.

How the Forecasting Model Works
Plain-language breakdown of what it does, how it learns, and what it costs.
school What Are These Tools?
StatsForecast, MLForecast (LightGBM wrapper), and HierarchicalForecast are Python libraries — free tools made by a company called Nixtla. Fully open-source (MIT license). No licensing fees, no subscriptions, no vendor relationship. You download them, you use them, you own everything that comes out of them. If Nixtla disappeared tomorrow, the libraries still work — the code is public, the community maintains them.
model_training Do We Train It Ourselves?
Yes — and that's the whole point. "Training" just means the model reads our historical sales data and learns the patterns. It's not magic, it's math running on our numbers.

Step 1: We feed it our historical sales data (orders by SKU, by day, by channel).
Step 2: The model finds patterns — "this SKU sells more on Mondays," "this product spikes every December," "when we run a promo, demand jumps 40%."
Step 3: It uses those patterns to predict future demand.

Every time we retrain (weekly), it gets our latest data and updates its understanding. The whole training process runs in minutes, not hours. It's a Python script — maybe 50-100 lines of code — running as a scheduled job on Azure Functions.
difference What's the Difference Between the Two Models?
StatsForecast looks at one thing: the sales history of that SKU over time. It sees the curve and projects it forward. "This SKU sold X last month, it'll probably sell Y next month." Works great for most products. Covers 80% of our catalog.

LightGBM looks at everything at once. You give it columns like: was there a promo running? What day of the week? What channel? What was the price? What was inventory level? What did marketing spend that week? It figures out which factors matter and by how much. So instead of "this SKU sold X, it'll sell Y," it's more like: "this SKU sold X, BUT there's a promo next week AND it's Q4 AND Amazon storage fees are about to spike, so it'll actually sell Z."

The more data we feed LightGBM, the smarter it gets. That's why pulling every inch of data from every system matters — every new signal we add improves accuracy. It also tells us which features actually drive demand, giving us business insights beyond just forecasts.
receipt_long What Does It Actually Cost to Run?
The libraries: $0. Open source. Free forever.

The compute to run them: almost nothing. An Azure Function running a Python forecasting script for ~1,000 SKUs takes a few minutes of compute. Azure Functions gives 1 million free executions per month. We'd use a few hundred. We're talking single-digit dollars per month, if anything.

The real cost is the data platform underneath — Azure SQL Database, Data Factory, Power BI. That's the $400-730/mo. The forecasting models themselves are essentially free to run on top of it.

We own everything. The predictions, the trained models, the code, the data — all ours. No vendor can raise prices, sunset a feature, or hold our data hostage.
autorenew What a Typical Forecasting Run Looks Like
A scheduled job fires (say, every Sunday night). It:

1. Pulls the last 2 years of sales data from Azure SQL
2. Feeds it to StatsForecast → baseline forecasts for all SKUs (seconds)
3. Feeds the top SKUs + all features (promos, price, channel, seasonality) to LightGBM → refined forecasts (minutes)
4. Runs HierarchicalForecast to reconcile Shopify + Amazon + Wholesale so they add up
5. Writes predictions back to Azure SQL
6. Power BI picks them up on next refresh — dashboards updated Monday morning

Total runtime: minutes. Total compute cost: pennies. Every week the model learns from the newest data and gets a little smarter.

factory

Deacom ERP (On-Premises SQL Server)

Very High

The hardest integration. Deacom runs on-premises — we need to bridge it to the cloud without disrupting production operations.

  • Access method: Read-only SQL queries against Deacom's SQL Server database. Must use NOLOCK hints to prevent record locking that could halt production.
  • ECI dependency: ECI (Deacom's vendor) controls API access. If direct SQL read isn't possible, we'd need ECI to enable their API server — adding timeline risk.
  • Schema discovery: Deacom's database schema is not publicly documented. We'll need to map key tables: items, inventory, BOMs, work orders, sales orders, raw materials, lot tracking.
  • Bridge to cloud: Azure Data Factory's Self-Hosted Integration Runtime installs on-prem and securely pushes data to Azure over HTTPS (port 443 outbound only). Battle-tested pattern for on-prem SQL Server connectivity.
  • Incremental loads: Full extracts would be too slow. Must implement change-detection (timestamp-based or change tracking) for near-real-time sync.
  • Performance monitoring: Any query that slows Deacom is unacceptable. Need query profiling and off-hours scheduling for heavy loads.
Risk: High — ECI gatekeeping, schema unknowns Prerequisite: Confirm read-only SQL access with ECI
shopping_cart

Shopify (DTC Channel)

Medium

API key acquired. Ready to build.

  • Historical backfill: Use GraphQL Bulk Operations — single async request, Shopify processes server-side, returns JSONL download. No rate limit issues. Best for 2+ years of order history.
  • Ongoing sync: Register webhooks for orders/create, inventory_levels/update. Daily polling as safety net for missed events.
  • Critical scope: Must verify read_all_orders is enabled — without it, only 60 days of order history is accessible.
  • Available data: Orders (with line items, quantities, pricing, discounts), Products & Variants, Customers, Collections, Inventory Levels, Marketing Events, Webhooks.

High

Complex async workflow. Amazon data is notoriously messy.

psychology 3. ML / Forecasting Engine High

layers Tiered Model Strategy
Not every SKU deserves the same model. Match complexity to importance:

Tier 1 — StatsForecast (80% of SKUs): Fast statistical baselines. AutoETS for standard SKUs, CrostonOptimized for slow/intermittent movers (long-tail). Fits thousands of series in seconds.

Tier 2 — LightGBM via MLForecast (Top 20% by revenue): Gradient-boosted models with rich features — promotional flags, day-of-week, channel indicators, price sensitivity, seasonality. Auto-generates lag features and rolling stats.

Tier 3 — HierarchicalForecast (Cross-channel): Ensures channel-level forecasts (Shopify + Amazon + Wholesale) sum coherently to totals. Prevents the common problem where individual channel forecasts don't add up.

warning Why This Is So Hard
Acme is a hand manufacturer at [Revenue Redacted] revenue. That means production capacity is real and constrained — by labor, equipment, batch sizes, and facility limits. A purely demand-driven forecast that ignores these constraints is fantasy planning. The system must always show "will it sell?" alongside "can we make it?" — and flag conflicts.
help What We Don't Know Yet — Production Time & Cost Per Product
A critical unknown: we don't currently have precise data on how long each product takes to manufacture or the true labor cost per unit. We know batch sizes and ingredient costs, but the time a worker spends per product — and therefore the labor cost per unit — is not being tracked at a granular level.

This is exactly the kind of data the platform needs to capture and surface. By integrating Deacom work order data with labor tracking, we can start measuring:
- Time per batch by product — how long does each production run actually take?
- Labor cost per unit — worker hours * hourly rate / units produced = true labor cost
- True COGS — ingredients + labor + overhead = actual cost to produce, not an estimate
- Production efficiency trends — are we getting faster or slower? Which products are labor-intensive vs. efficient?
- Capacity planning accuracy — without knowing how long products take to make, we can't accurately forecast production capacity

This data gap means our current margin calculations are incomplete. We may think a product is profitable when the labor cost makes it marginal, or vice versa. The data lake should be designed from the start to capture production time data so we can close this gap and make forecasting truly cost-aware.
account_tree BOM Explosion
Translate demand forecasts into ingredient requirements using Bills of Material from Deacom. If we forecast 10,000 units of Product A, how much of each raw material do we need? This feeds into procurement planning and surfaces ingredient shortages before they become production delays.
groups Production Capacity
Model labor hours, equipment throughput, and batch sizes from Deacom. Hand manufacturing has variable production times and minimum batch sizes. Changeover between products costs time. The forecast output must be checked against these constraints — if we can't make 10,000 units in the time window, the system needs to flag it.
eco Ingredient Sourcing & Seasonality
Organic/natural raw materials have seasonal availability windows. Some ingredients can only be sourced at certain times of year. Supplier lead times vary — domestic vs. imported. The forecasting system must factor in: which ingredients are available when, supplier lead times, and whether projected demand requires ingredients that are out of season. This data needs to be maintained as a raw material availability calendar, either manually or from Deacom purchasing data.
timer

Shelf Life & FEFO Modeling

Organic products expire. This creates a forecasting dimension most tools ignore entirely.

  • > Lot-level tracking: Every production batch has an expiration date from Deacom. The warehouse must store this per-lot.
  • > FEFO inventory allocation: First Expired, First Out — not standard FIFO. When fulfilling orders across channels, ship the lots closest to expiration first.
  • > Waste forecasting: If projected sell-through rate is slower than shelf life, flag it. Alert: "Batch X of Product Y will expire before it sells at current velocity."
  • > Retail channel sensitivity: Wholesale partners may require minimum shelf life remaining at time of shipment (e.g., "must have 6+ months remaining"). This constrains which lots can go to which channels.
  • > Overproduction prevention: Don't forecast production that exceeds what can sell before expiration. This is the intersection of demand forecasting and shelf life math.
output
Deacom MRP Feed: The final output of the forecasting system must flow back into Deacom in a format its MRP module can consume. This closes the loop: forecast demand → check constraints → generate production schedule → feed to Deacom MRP. The forecast should complement Deacom's built-in forecasting (smoothing, percentage increases), not replace it entirely — Deacom's MRP handles forecast consumption and offset logic.

Forecasting Model: The Nixtla Stack
A three-tier approach that matches model complexity to SKU importance.
lightbulb Why This Stack
Not every SKU deserves the same model. Running a complex ML model on a SKU that sells 3 units a month is a waste of compute — a simple statistical method works fine. But our top 20% revenue-driving SKUs need every signal we can throw at them. The Nixtla stack lets us tier our approach: simple where simple works, sophisticated where it matters.
TIER 1 StatsForecast — The Baseline (80% of SKUs)
What it is: A library of proven statistical forecasting models — AutoARIMA, AutoETS, AutoTheta, CrostonOptimized, and more. These are the methods that have been forecasting demand for decades. Fast, reliable, no training data requirements beyond the time series itself.

Why it's right for the long tail: 80% of our SKUs are lower volume. They don't have enough data density for complex ML to outperform statistics. AutoETS handles standard SKUs with seasonal patterns. CrostonOptimized is specifically designed for intermittent/slow-moving items (a SKU that sells 0, 0, 5, 0, 0, 3, 0...). This is common in organic product lines with niche products.

Performance: Fits thousands of SKU time series in seconds. Not minutes, not hours — seconds. This means we can retrain every SKU every day if we want to, with negligible compute cost.

What it needs: Historical sales data by SKU by time period. Minimum: 2 years weekly data (104 data points per SKU). More is better for seasonality detection.
TIER 2 LightGBM via MLForecast — The Power Model (Top 20% by Revenue)
What it is: LightGBM is a gradient-boosted decision tree algorithm — one of the most powerful and widely-used ML algorithms for tabular data. MLForecast is the Nixtla wrapper that makes it work for time-series forecasting by automatically generating lag features, rolling statistics, and date features.

Why it's right for top SKUs: This is where pulling every inch of data pays off. LightGBM can ingest dozens of features simultaneously and figure out which ones matter. The more signals we feed it, the better it gets:

Sales Signals
Historical velocity, order frequency, units per order, return rates, channel source (Shopify vs Amazon vs wholesale)
Time Features
Day of week, month, week of year, holiday flags, quarter, payday cycles, seasonal patterns
Business Signals
Price changes, promotional flags, marketing spend, influencer campaigns, new product launches, inventory levels
Channel-Specific
Amazon FBA restock timing, storage fee windows, Shopify traffic patterns, wholesale reorder cycles from 400+ partners
Feature importance: LightGBM tells you which features actually drive predictions. We'll learn things like "promotions increase demand by 35% for this SKU" or "wholesale orders spike every 90 days" — insights that inform business decisions beyond just forecasting.

Performance: Trains in minutes, not hours. Predictions are near-instant. Can be retrained daily as new data flows in.
TIER 3 HierarchicalForecast — Multi-Channel Reconciliation
The problem it solves: If we forecast Shopify, Amazon, and wholesale independently, those numbers won't add up. We might forecast 1,000 units on Shopify, 800 on Amazon, and 500 wholesale — but the product-level total forecast says 2,100. That's a 200-unit discrepancy. Which number does production trust? Which one do we make?

How it works: HierarchicalForecast takes the individual channel forecasts and reconciles them so they're mathematically consistent. Channel forecasts sum to the product total. Product totals sum to the category total. Category totals sum to the company total. Every level is coherent.

Why it matters for manufacturing: Production planning needs one number per SKU: how many to make. If different channels give different answers and they don't reconcile, the production planner is guessing. Hierarchical reconciliation gives one trustworthy number that accounts for all channels.

Hierarchy for Acme:
Total Company → Product Category → Individual SKU → SKU by Channel (Shopify / Amazon FBA / Amazon FBM / Wholesale)

Meta's Prophet was the go-to forecasting library for years, but it's now in maintenance mode — no active development. Multiple benchmarks show it underperforming the Nixtla stack (StatsForecast, MLForecast) in both accuracy and speed. NeuralProphet is the successor and is worth keeping an eye on for future experimentation, but LightGBM with well-engineered features will likely match or beat it for our use case. We're not dismissing it entirely — it's just not the best choice for a new build in 2026.

report_problem The Deacom Problem
No vendor has a native Deacom connector. No matter which tool we choose, Deacom integration is custom work. Integration paths:

1. Direct SQL Read — If ECI allows it. Most control, lowest ongoing cost. Requires NOLOCK hints and careful query design.
2. ECI REST API — ECI must install/enable the API server. Adds dependency on ECI's timeline and support.
3. Middleware (Celigo, Workato, Boomi) — $500-2,000/mo. Adds another vendor dependency. May still need custom connector development.
4. CSV/Flat File Export — Fallback. Deacom exports scheduled files that get picked up by the pipeline. Lowest tech barrier, but introduces latency and fragility.

Bottom line: Even with a $50K/year SaaS forecasting tool, you still need custom Deacom integration work. This is why building custom is more compelling — you're doing the hard integration regardless.
